# Introduction to UX and UI

## 1. Course Context

Little Lemon is a restaurant that has a website, but the number of online food orders and table reservations is low. Adrian, one of the co-owners, realizes that the current interface and experience are no longer effective, even though he still likes the old style. As a result, a decision is made to redesign the website.

This project is an opportunity to:

* Learn and apply UX/UI principles
* Understand how UX and UI are used in real-world scenarios
* Explore career opportunities in digital product design

---

## 2. Lesson Objectives

After this lesson, it will be possible to:

1. Clearly distinguish between UX (User Experience) and UI (User Interface)
2. Understand the roles of UX and UI in the product design lifecycle
3. Identify UX/UI roles in both small and large companies

---

## 3. What Is UX? (User Experience)

### 3.1 Definition of UX

UX is not just the interface of a website or an application. UX is the entire experience a customer has with a business, including:

* Staff
* Service
* Food
* Space and decoration
* Menu
* Restrooms
* Website and applications

In this course, UX focuses on the process of designing and redesigning the Little Lemon website.

### 3.2 What UX Focuses On

UX is the process of asking questions and finding answers, such as:

* What are customers‚Äô real needs?
* What prevents users from ordering food or making reservations?
* Is the website easy to use?
* Can users place orders quickly?
* Is the transition between steps (choosing dishes, customizing, checkout) smooth?

UX ensures that a product is:

* Easy to understand
* Easy to use
* Efficient in helping users achieve their goals

---

## 4. What Is UI? (User Interface)

### 4.1 Definition of UI

UI refers to everything users see and directly interact with, including:

* Typography
* Colors
* Buttons
* Shapes
* Icons
* Images

The Little Lemon website is the main touchpoint between users and the brand.

### 4.2 Role of UI

UI focuses on:

* Logical arrangement of visual elements
* Clear guidance for user actions
* Visual appeal
* Consistency with brand identity

A good UI helps users:

* Immediately understand what to do
* Complete tasks quickly
* Feel comfortable while using the product

---

## 5. The Relationship Between UX and UI

UX and UI are closely connected:

* UX identifies problems and user needs
* UX creates prototypes and proposes solutions
* UI turns those solutions into visual, attractive, and usable interfaces

When combined effectively:

* The product is both functional and appealing
* The experience becomes smooth and enjoyable

---

## 6. UX/UI Roles in Different Types of Companies

### 6.1 Small Companies

* One person may handle both UX and UI
* Responsibilities may include:

  * User research
  * Solution design
  * Interface design
  * Possibly participating in coding

### 6.2 Large Companies

Roles are more specialized, for example:

* UX Researcher: user research
* UX Copywriter: content, tone, and wording
* UI Designer (Design System): managing the design system
* Accessibility Designer: ensuring accessibility for users with disabilities

## 2. What Is UX?

# UX Goals and Usability

## 1. Identifying the Right Problem

After discussions with Adrian, it becomes clear that:

* Customers cannot easily make reservations or place orders
* This is a **usability** issue

‚Üí It is not just about visual appearance.

UX/UI must:

* Understand customer frustration
* Align user goals with business goals

---

## 2. What Is Usability?

Usability measures whether a product is:

* Easy to learn
* Easy to use
* Error-resistant
* Satisfying

A product with good usability:

* Reduces confusion
* Lowers abandonment rates
* Increases task completion rates (reservations, orders)

---

## 3. Jakob Nielsen‚Äôs 5 Usability Components

### 3.1 Learnability

* Is it easy to understand on first use?
* Is the ordering/reservation flow clear?

Example:

* Can a first-time user successfully place an order?

### 3.2 Efficiency

* Once learned, is the process fast?
* Is editing an order easy?

Example:

* Changing a dish in just a few steps
* No need to restart the process

### 3.3 Memorability

* Can users resume easily after a break?
* Do they remember where they left off?

Example:

* Returning to the reservation page and remembering the selected date

### 3.4 Errors

* Are errors easy to make?
* When errors occur:

  * Are warnings clear?
  * Are recovery options available?

Example:

* Entering an invalid email ‚Üí specific error message
* Preventing errors before form submission

### 3.5 Satisfaction

* Is the experience pleasant?
* Does it feel comfortable and enjoyable?

Note:

* Satisfaction is hard to measure
* But it is easy to recognize when a product ‚Äúfeels good‚Äù to use

---

## 4. Applying Usability Early to Avoid Problems

When usability is considered early:

* Problems are detected sooner
* Fixing costs are reduced
* Wrong design directions are avoided

Good UX:

* Solves problems before they become serious

## 6. Supportive but Non-Intrusive Design

A good design should be:

Examples in Little Lemon:

## 7. Considering Accessibility

If direct support is not possible:

* Information must be easy to find
* A way to note special requests should be provided

## UX Process and How to Apply It to Little Lemon

### 1. UX Is a Process

UX is a process-centered discipline.
There is no single model that fits every project, but following the core steps helps to:

* Design according to real user needs
* Increase revenue
* Retain returning customers

The UX process is **iterative**, meaning it is possible to return to previous stages to adjust and improve the design.

---

### 2. The 5 Stages of the UX Process

#### 1. Empathize ‚Äì Understanding Users

**Goals**

* Understand users‚Äô needs, behaviors, and pain points
* Avoid designing based on assumptions

**Applied to Little Lemon**

* Ask for permission to interview and observe customers
* Observe how they:

  * Place orders
  * Make reservations
  * Find restaurant information
* Listen to frustrations and barriers when using the website

**Outputs**

* User Persona
* Empathy Map
* Scenario
* User Journey Map

These tools ensure that all design ideas stay grounded in real user needs.

---

#### 2. Define ‚Äì Defining the Problem

**Goals**

* Synthesize and refine collected data
* Identify the core problems to solve

**Key Activities**

* Group user problems
* Prioritize pain points by importance
* Clearly answer:

  * Who are the users?
  * What difficulties do they face?
  * Which problem should be solved first?

**Output**

* User Needs Statement

---

#### 3. Ideate ‚Äì Generating Ideas

**Goals**

* Generate as many solutions as possible
* Avoid rushing to select a single solution

**Methods**

* Sketching
* Brainstorming
* Mind mapping
* Handwritten notes

**Key Principles**

* Maintain an open mindset
* Be ready to iterate and refine ideas

---

#### 4. Prototype ‚Äì Creating Prototypes

**Wireframe**

* 2D design layout
* Focuses on:

  * Layout
  * Content hierarchy
  * Functionality
  * User actions

**Prototype**

* A simulation close to the real product
* Includes:

  * Colors
  * Buttons
  * Text
  * Interactions

**Prototypes help to:**

* Validate ideas before actual development
* Simulate real-world usage scenarios

---

#### 5. Test ‚Äì Usability Testing

**Goals**

* Verify whether the design is truly effective
* Detect issues before development

**How to Conduct Testing**

* Prepare a test script with clear tasks
* Users perform tasks on the prototype
* Observe and record:

  * Difficulties
  * Confusion
  * Frustration

**After Testing**

* Revise the design
* Continue iterating to improve the experience

---

### 6. Build ‚Äì Development

After:

* Fully understanding users
* Testing and refining solutions
* Confirming the effectiveness of the solution

‚Üí Proceed to build the real product.

Following the full process ensures:

* The website solves the right problems
* A smooth and intuitive experience
* Higher conversion rates and customer retention

---

### 7. Summary

* UX is a process, not a single step
* 5 stages:

  1. Empathize
  2. Define
  3. Ideate
  4. Prototype
  5. Test
* The process is iterative
* All design decisions are user-centered

## What Is UI? The Role of UI in UX/UI Design

### 1. What Is UI?

UI (User Interface) is what people see and directly interact with when using a digital product such as a website or application.

UI includes:

* Information layout
* Colors
* Typography
* Icons
* Images
* Buttons, forms, menus
* Feedback on click, tap, and hover

In short:
**UI is how humans communicate with technology.**

---

### 2. What Does ‚ÄúInterface‚Äù Mean?

An interface refers to interaction or communication.

Humans interact with:

* Other humans (email, messages)
* Machines and technology (phones, printers, websites)

Examples:

* Adjusting printer settings
* Ordering a drink and tracking the preparation process
* Ordering food on the Little Lemon website

All of these are forms of interaction through an interface.

---

### 3. Graphical User Interface (GUI)

GUI is the most common type of interface today, using:

* Images
* Icons
* Text
* Colors
* Visual space

On the Little Lemon website, GUI includes:

* Menu page
* Reservation page
* Ordering page
* ‚ÄúOrder,‚Äù ‚ÄúReserve,‚Äù and ‚ÄúCheckout‚Äù buttons

---

### 4. UI Is Not Just What You See

UI is not only static visuals.
It also includes:

* What happens when a button is clicked
* Feedback after user actions
* Loading, error, and success states
* How the system responds to users

Examples:

* Clicking ‚ÄúReserve a Table‚Äù ‚Üí confirmation message
* No tables available ‚Üí clear and understandable notification

---

### 5. UI Is Task-Oriented

**Real-Life Examples**

* Car dashboard:

  * Press the accelerator ‚Üí speed indicator increases
  * Warning lights ‚Üí feedback for the driver
* Mobile phone:

  * Battery and Wi-Fi indicators ‚Üí system status communication

UI helps people:

* Understand what is happening
* Know what action to take next

---

### 6. Importance of UI

Well-designed UI can:

* Enable fast and accurate interaction
* Reduce errors
* Increase trust
* Even save lives

Examples:

* Medical devices displaying vital signs
* Aircraft cockpits in poor weather conditions

For Little Lemon:

* Poor UI ‚Üí users abandon orders
* Good UI ‚Üí increased orders and reservations

## Types of Digital Design

### 1. Overview

As design evolves, terminology expands. To learn UX/UI effectively, it is important to understand where UX and UI sit within the **design spectrum** and how they relate to other design types.

There are four main groups:

1. Service Design
2. Customer Experience Design (CX)
3. User Experience Design (UX)
4. User Interface Design (UI)

---

### 2. Service Design

**Definition**
Service Design covers the entire service experience, including:

* Digital components
* Non-digital elements (offline experiences, people, processes)

It considers every touchpoint between a business and its customers.

**Little Lemon Example**
Service Design includes:

* Reservation website
* Service staff
* Customer greeting process
* Restaurant space
* Complaint handling

üëâ This is the broadest level.

---

### 3. Customer Experience Design (CX)

**Definition**
CX focuses on:

* The overall experience with a brand
* All interactions before, during, and after using a service

**Difference from Service Design**

* Service Design: designs the entire service system
* CX: focuses on customer perception and journey with the brand

**Little Lemon Example**
CX includes:

* Brand impression
* Emotions during reservation
* Feelings after dining
* Likelihood of returning or recommending

---

### 4. User Experience Design (UX)

**Definition**
UX focuses on:

* All interactions between users and a specific touchpoint
* The overall feeling during use

‚ö†Ô∏è UX is not visual design.

UX considers:

* Ease of use
* Frustration levels
* Goal achievement
* Emotional response

**Examples**
If users:

* Cannot easily make reservations
* Do not know which dishes are available
  ‚Üí These are UX problems.

---

### 5. User Interface Design (UI)

**Definition**
UI focuses on:

* Pixel-perfect digital interfaces
* Colors
* Typography
* Icons
* Buttons
* Layout
* Interaction states

UI answers:

* What does the solution look like and how is it interacted with?

**UX‚ÄìUI Relationship**

* UX defines problems and experiences
* UI visualizes UX solutions

üëâ UI must serve UX and cannot exist independently.

---

### 6. User-Centered Design (UCD)

**Definition**
UCD is an iterative process where:

* User needs
* Desires
* Limitations

are considered at every design stage.

**Core Principle**
Users are at the center of all design decisions.

**Benefits**

* Clear understanding of real user needs
* Avoids subjective design decisions
* Reduces the risk of product failure

---

### 7. Design Thinking

**Definition**
Design Thinking is a creative problem-solving approach based on:

* A structured process
* Encouragement of creativity

Although it seems contradictory, constraints and structure actually enhance creativity.

---

### 8. The 5 Steps of Design Thinking

1. **Empathize**

   * Understand users
   * Observe and interview
   * Listen to frustrations

   Example:
   ‚ÄúOrdering this takes too much time.‚Äù

2. **Define**

   * Synthesize data
   * Identify core problems

   Example:
   Users need to quickly understand the menu to decide what to order.

3. **Ideate**

   * Brainstorm
   * Sketch
   * Avoid limiting early ideas

4. **Prototype**

   * Wireframes
   * Sketches
   * Low- or high-fidelity prototypes

5. **Test**

   * Test with real users
   * Observe behavior
   * Identify issues
   * Iterate and improve

‚Üí This is an **iterative**, not linear, process.

---

### 9. Summary

* Service Design: entire service system
* CX: brand experience
* UX: experience at a specific touchpoint
* UI: visual and interactive interface
* UCD: user-centered approach
* Design Thinking: creative problem-solving framework
* 5 steps: Empathize ‚Üí Define ‚Üí Ideate ‚Üí Prototype ‚Üí Test

# DESIGNING FOR ACCESSIBILITY

## 1. What Is Accessibility?

Accessibility enables people with disabilities to:

* Perceive
* Understand
* Navigate
* Interact
* Contribute to the web

Includes people who are:

* Blind, color-blind, or have low vision
* Deaf or hard of hearing
* Have limited mobility (temporary or permanent)
* Have cognitive difficulties

üëâ Good design is not only for ‚Äúpeople like us,‚Äù but for as many people as possible.

---

## 2. Correct Understanding of Accessibility Design

* Accessibility does **not** make products ugly or boring
* Accessibility creates **positive design constraints**
* Constraints help to:

  * Reduce errors
  * Improve usability
  * Benefit all users

üëâ Accessible design = smarter design

---

## 3. Do Not Use Color Alone to Convey Information

**Incorrect:**

* Using red only to indicate errors
* Using green only to distinguish states

**Correct:**

* Combine color + icon + text
* Examples:

  * Required field: asterisk (*) + label + color
  * Charts: color + symbols + labels

**Why:**

* 1 in 12 men is color-blind
* Many people have low vision or are completely blind

---

## 4. Ensure Sufficient Contrast

* Text against background: minimum **4.5 : 1**
* Large text:

  * ‚â• 24px normal
  * ‚â• 19px bold
    ‚Üí can be reduced to **3 : 1**

Applies to:

* Text
* Buttons
* Icons
* Text on images / gradients

Does not apply to:

* Logos
* Text embedded in decorative images

---

## 5. Interactive Elements Must Be Clearly Identifiable

Interactive elements include:

* Buttons
* Links
* Menus
* Clickable icons

Requirements:

* Clearly look clickable
* Clear states:

  * Hover
  * Focus (keyboard)
  * Active

Especially important for people who:

* Do not use a mouse
* Rely only on the keyboard

üëâ Always provide a clear keyboard focus (outline, highlight)

---

## 6. Clear Form Labels

* Every input field needs a descriptive label
* Label placement:

  * To the left
  * Or above the input field
* Avoid placing labels too far from inputs

Exceptions:

* Checkboxes
* Radio buttons (labels usually on the right)

üëâ Do not rely on placeholders alone

---

## 7. Clear and Consistent Navigation

* Consistent naming
* Consistent navigation placement
* Provide multiple navigation methods:

  * Menu
  * Search
  * Sitemap
  * Breadcrumbs

Goal:

* Users always know:

  * Where they are
  * Where they can go next

---

## 8. Provide Clear Feedback

Feedback is needed when:

* A form is successfully submitted
* An error occurs
* An important action is completed

Good feedback:

* Highly visible
* Clear language
* Actionable guidance (not just error messages)

Examples:

* Error lists
* Icons + background color
* Messages placed near the error location

---

## 9. Use Headings and White Space to Group Content

* Headings help:

  * Fast scanning
  * Understanding content structure
* White space helps:

  * Show relationships
  * Reduce cognitive load

üëâ Especially important for:

* People with attention difficulties
* Screen reader users

---

## 10. Design for Multiple Screen Sizes

Must be tested on:

* Mobile
* Desktop
* High zoom levels (for low vision)

Pay attention to:

* Font size
* Line length
* Position of headings and navigation

üëâ Not only responsive, but readable

---

## 11. Auto-Playing Content Must Have Controls

Applies to:

* Carousels
* Videos
* Audio
* Auto-running animations

Requirements:

* Pause button
* Clear controls
* No sudden or distracting behavior

---

## 12. Conclusion

* Designing for accessibility = responsible design
* The better users are understood ‚Üí the better the experience
* Accessibility is not only for people with disabilities, but for everyone

# Form Design and Best Practices in UX/UI

## 1. The Role of Design Principles in UX/UI

Good design principles are often combined with best practices when creating digital products.
Even very simple components such as forms can cause frustration if they are not designed with user experience in mind.

In this lesson, login and food-ordering forms from the Little Lemon website are used as examples to illustrate the importance of proper form design.

---

## 2. Why Form Design Matters

Forms are the direct point of interaction between users and the system.
If a form is poorly designed, users may:

* Misunderstand the questions
* Enter incorrect information
* Abandon the process halfway

Analyzing forms based on:

* Usability
* User goals

helps create logical, user-centered solutions that feel familiar to most people.

---

## 3. Design Thinking Based on Best Practices

Every interface element‚Äîsimple or complex‚Äîfollows the same foundational rules:

* Clarity
* Ease of understanding
* Consistency

Regularly reviewing and applying best practices helps build correct design thinking from the beginning.
The course also provides additional materials and detailed guidance on form design best practices.

---

## 4. Using User Feedback to Improve Forms

After collecting feedback through interviews and observation, the form was redesigned in HTML for testing.
An experienced UX/UI designer (Enzo) was invited to review the prototype and provide feedback.

---

## 5. Identified Issues and Improvements

### 5.1. Unclear Questions and Input Fields

Issues:

* Some questions were difficult to understand
* Input fields were placed too close together

Solutions:

* Increase spacing between fields such as text inputs, password fields, checkboxes, and radio buttons
* Help users clearly distinguish each field and its corresponding label

---

### 5.2. Lack of Logical Information Grouping

Issue:

* Information sections were mixed together, causing confusion

Solutions:

* Group information logically (e.g., address, payment)
* Use subtle background colors or dividers to separate groups

---

### 5.3. No Awareness of Progress in the Process

Issues:

* No visible progress
* Unclear how many steps remained
* No way to return to previous steps

Solutions:

* Add a progress indicator
* Allow users to go back and review or edit entered information

---

### 5.4. Late and Unclear Error Messages

Issues:

* Errors appeared only after the entire form was completed
* Errors did not clearly indicate what was wrong or where

Solutions:

* Validate input as users type (inline validation)
* Display clear, contextual error messages for each section

---

### 5.5. Password Requirements Not Explained

Issues:

* The system required complex passwords
* Criteria were not clearly stated

Solutions:

* Clearly display password requirements
* Add a password strength indicator
* Provide immediate feedback while typing

---

## 6. The Value of Feedback in UX/UI Design

Collecting feedback from others helps to:

* Identify issues early
* Improve accessibility
* Make forms easier to understand and use

---

## 7. Conclusion

In this lesson, the following were achieved:

* Understanding the importance of clear form design
* Applying best practices to improve usability
* Using clear layouts, proper field spacing, and logical information grouping
* Aligning business goals with user goals

The result is a product that is closer to a successful UX/UI design.

# Component-Driven Design and Design Systems

## 1. What Is Component-Driven Design?

In modern product design, many teams use component-driven design to build interfaces more efficiently and consistently.
This approach improves components on the Little Lemon website and helps create attractive, user-friendly interfaces.

---

## 2. Components in UI Design

In UI design, a component is:

* A part of the interface
* Logically grouped
* Able to function independently
* Reusable

Components can be small (buttons) or large (headers, forms).

---

## 3. How to Build Components

The component-building process usually includes three steps:

1. **Start with small components**

   * Examples: buttons, search fields, menu items

2. **Combine small components into complex components**

   * Example: a header containing a logo, navigation bar, and reservation button

3. **Use components to create complete interfaces**

   * Entire pages are assembled from components

---

## 4. Real-World Example: Website Header

Headers appear on many pages of a website.
Instead of redesigning the header for each page:

* Create a shared header component
* The header contains child components such as:

  * Buttons
  * Search bars
  * Navigation bars

These child components remain independent and reusable elsewhere.

---

## 5. Benefits of Component-Driven Design

Designing interfaces using modular building blocks provides many benefits:

* Faster design and development
* Easier creation of variations for different actions
* Consistent interfaces
* Easier for users to learn and remember
* Better overall visual experience

---

## 6. What Is a Design System?

A design system is:

* A collection of pre-designed components and patterns
* Used to build products at scale
* Similar to a brand style guide

A design system includes:

* Rules
* Behaviors
* Best practices for design components

---

## 7. Common Parts of a Design System

There is no fixed checklist, but successful design systems usually include:

### 7.1. Design Guidelines

* Reflect brand values and identity
* Define color, typography, spacing, and tone of voice

---

### 7.2. Design Pattern Library

* A collection of widely accepted and reused design patterns
* According to the Interaction Design Foundation:
  A pattern is a repetition of multiple design elements working together
* Elements may include shapes, lines, and colors

---

### 7.3. UI Kit / Component Library

* A collection of UI components
* Examples: buttons, inputs, widgets, modals
* Enables fast and consistent interface creation

---

### 7.4. Process Design Guidelines

* Guidance on the design process
* Helps apply design principles correctly during work
* Supports consistent design decision-making

---

# Using Menu Icons in Navigation

## 1. What Are Menu Icons?

Menu icons are small graphics representing:

* A menu
* A group of actions
* A navigation area

They commonly appear on websites and applications, especially on mobile devices due to limited screen space.

There is no single official naming standard, as systems like Font Awesome and Material Design use different naming conventions.

---

## 2. The Role of Navigation Icons

Navigation icons help:

* Users find content faster
* Reduce interface complexity
* Optimize screen space

They are one of the primary tools for content discovery in apps and websites.

---

## 3. Common Types of Menu Icons

### 3.1. Hamburger Menu

**Description**

* Three horizontal lines of equal length
* Resembles a hamburger (two buns and a patty)

**Usage**

* Usually placed in the top or bottom corner
* Opens the main navigation menu
* Suitable for mobile interfaces or menus with many items

**Note**

* Should be used sparingly on desktop interfaces, as it can reduce content discoverability

---

### 3.2. Kebab Menu

**Description**

* Three vertical dots
* Originated from Google‚Äôs Material Design

**Usage**

* Opens a menu with additional options
* Usually placed in the top-right or top-left corner
* Suitable for general actions

---

### 3.3. Meatballs Menu

**Description**

* Three horizontal dots

**Usage**

* Opens an action menu for a specific item
* Common in tables or lists
* Suitable for horizontal layouts

---

### 3.4. Bento Menu

**Description**

* Grid-style icon
* Inspired by Japanese bento boxes

**Usage**

* Switches between apps, features, or sections within a product
* Common in large product ecosystems

---

### 3.5. D√∂ner Menu

**Description**

* Three horizontal lines of varying lengths
* Commonly interpreted as a filter icon

**Usage**

* Represents filtering options
* Often paired with ‚Äúsort by‚Äù functionality
* Popular in modern applications

---

## 4. Other Menu Icon Variations

In addition to common types, there are also:

* Candy box
* Cheeseburger
* Veggie burger
* Other icon variations

These icons often carry very specific meanings.
When using them, context and purpose must be clear to avoid confusion.

---

## 5. Principles for Using Menu Icons

* Choose icons that match their function
* Maintain consistency throughout the product
* Avoid overusing icons that may confuse users
* Combine icons with text labels when clarification is needed

---

## 6. Conclusion

Menu icons are small elements but play a critical role in:

* Navigation
* Usability
* Overall user experience

Clearly explaining meaning and using icons in the right context helps reduce disorientation and improves interaction efficiency on websites and applications.
